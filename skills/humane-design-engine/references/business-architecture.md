# Business Architecture: Anatomy of Market-Ready Digital Products

This document teaches the anatomy of pages that convert. Every section has a business job. Design should serve that job — not decorate around it.

Use this when planning page layouts, evaluating what sections to include, and deciding visual hierarchy.

---

## Part 1: The Anatomy of a Market-Ready Product Page

Every page section exists to answer a specific question the visitor has at that moment in their journey. Understand the question, then design the answer.

---

### Section 1: Hero / Value Proposition

**Business job:** Answer "what is this and why should I care?" in under 5 seconds.

**What it needs:**
- Headline: the core benefit (not the feature)
- Subheadline: how it delivers that benefit
- Primary CTA: one action, clearly stated
- Visual: demonstrating the product in use, not a decorative illustration

**Conversion science:**
- The hero is where 80% of visitors decide to stay or bounce. It earns everything that follows.
- Benefit-first headlines outperform feature headlines. "Ship faster" > "5000 curated rules"
- First-person CTAs outperform by ~25%: "Start building" > "Get started"
- One CTA wins over two. If you must have two, one is clearly primary.

**Common mistakes:**
- Vague headlines ("The future of AI development")
- Multiple competing CTAs of equal weight
- Stock photos or generic illustrations instead of product visuals
- Industry jargon assumed to be understood ("Rules-as-code for your agentic workflow")

**For RuleSell:** The quality score is the brand's visual heart. Find a way to make it present, even in the hero.

---

### Section 2: Social Proof Bar

**Business job:** Reduce the visitor's uncertainty immediately after the hero. You've made a claim — now show evidence.

**What it needs:**
- Logos of recognizable companies/tools using the product
- Aggregate metrics: "50,000+ developers", "10,000+ rules installed"
- Press mentions or awards if available

**Conversion science:**
- 87% of consumers read reviews before purchasing (BrightLocal)
- Social proof can increase page conversion by up to 34%
- Immediate proof after hero catches the bounce-intenders

**Design principles:**
- 4–6 logos, not 12
- Slightly desaturated — never louder than the product
- Horizontal bar format, as a divider between hero and problem
- Quiet confidence, not desperation

**Common mistakes:**
- Making logos too prominent (brand parade instead of trust signal)
- Including logos of companies visitors have never heard of
- Fake or unverifiable metrics

---

### Section 3: Problem / Pain Statement

**Business job:** Make the visitor feel understood. If they feel understood, they trust what comes next.

**What it needs:**
- Pain articulation in the customer's language, not the company's language
- Specific, named problems — not "inefficiency" but "you spend an hour reformatting the same rules for every new project"
- Acknowledgment that this problem is real and frustrating

**Why it works:**
- Creates the gap that the features section will fill
- People buy solutions to problems they feel, not problems they understand intellectually
- Matching the visitor's internal monologue builds immediate connection

**Design:** This section can be quieter — text-forward, dark, introspective. It should feel like the product understands the developer's frustration before selling them anything.

**For RuleSell:** The problem is that AI tool rule sets are scattered, unverified, unmaintained, and impossible to evaluate for quality. Developers waste hours finding rules that may be outdated or broken.

---

### Section 4: Features / Benefits Section

**Business job:** Show HOW the product solves the problem you just named.

**What it needs:**
- 3–5 features, each expressed as a benefit plus visual proof
- Clear hierarchy: feature name prominent, benefit explanation supporting, visual confirming
- Scannable structure — someone skimming should still understand

**Conversion science:**
- Hick's Law: more choices = longer decisions = more drop-off. 3–5 features, not 15.
- Abstract features lose to concrete benefits
- Visual proof (screenshot, diagram, example output) alongside text doubles comprehension

**Design:** Alternating or grid layout. Each feature gets its moment. Not all equal weight — lead with the most compelling differentiator.

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing 15+ features (exhausting)
- Feature names without explaining the benefit ("API Access" — so what?)
- Abstract icons that don't communicate anything

**For RuleSell:** Lead with quality measurement. Then verified reviews. Then creator economics. Then breadth of asset types.

---

### Section 5: How It Works / Process Section

**Business job:** Reduce perceived complexity. A product that feels simple to start feels safe to try.

**What it needs:**
- 3–4 numbered steps showing the user's journey through the product
- Each step described in the user's action, not the system's behavior
- Approximate time or effort cue if appropriate ("In 2 minutes, you'll have...")

**Conversion science:**
- Reducing perceived effort increases conversion
- "How it works" sections signal that the product has a clear, teachable model — products without them feel opaque

**Design:** Linear, numbered, directional. Can use connectors, arrows, or progressive revelation. Should feel like inevitability — step 3 obviously follows step 2.

**For RuleSell:** Distinguish buyer and creator flows. Buyers: Search → Preview quality score → Install → Done. Creators: Upload → Verification → Publish → Earn.

---

### Section 6: Differentiation / Comparison Section

**Business job:** Answer the question "why this instead of just Googling/building it myself/using a competitor?"

**What it needs:**
- Honest comparison that highlights genuine differentiators
- Acknowledgment of what alternatives exist (shows confidence)
- Clear statement of what makes this product different in ways that matter

**Design:** Can be a comparison table, side-by-side, or narrative. Avoid the common trap of filling every column with checkmarks — be selective and specific.

**For RuleSell's specific differentiators:**
- "Quality is measured, not voted on" — objective scoring vs. star ratings
- "Creators keep 85%" — compared to most platform commission structures
- "Every item verified, not just reviewed" — VirusTotal scanning, Semgrep analysis, sandboxed installs
- "Verified-install reviews only" — no review gaming

---

### Section 7: Testimonials / Case Studies

**Business job:** Peer validation. A customer saying it is infinitely more credible than the product saying it.

**What it needs:**
- Real quotes with real names, roles, and company
- Photos (even small avatars) dramatically increase credibility
- Measurable outcomes in case studies ("reduced rule setup time by 60%")
- Range of voices — different roles, use cases, company sizes

**Design principles:**
- Feel editorial, not manufactured
- Different quote lengths and tones signal authentic voices
- Avoid the 3-column grid of identical testimonial cards — it looks staged

**Common mistakes:**
- Anonymous quotes or vague attribution ("Senior Developer")
- All testimonials praising the same feature
- Testimonials that sound like marketing copy wrote them

---

### Section 8: Pricing / Business Model Section

**Business job:** Remove price anxiety. Make the cost feel obvious, fair, and safe.

**What it needs:**
- Clear pricing with inclusions listed (what do I actually get?)
- Highlighted recommended tier
- Yearly/monthly toggle if applicable
- FAQ or clarification for the most common objections

**Conversion science:**
- Anchor pricing: show a higher tier first to frame middle as reasonable
- Highlight the "most popular" plan — social proof + decision shortcut
- Free tier (if it exists) should clearly show how users graduate to paid

**For RuleSell:** The model is simple — free assets are free, paid assets have 15% commission, Pro is $8/month for creators. Let the simplicity speak. A complex pricing page for a simple model signals organizational confusion.

**Design:** The highlighted plan should be visually unmistakable. Don't use subtle differences — commit to the contrast.

---

### Section 9: Trust & Security Section

**Business job:** Address the final "can I trust this?" objection. Especially critical for developer tools where malicious code is a real risk.

**What it needs:**
- Security verification process explained plainly
- Badges and certifications where genuine
- Privacy policy and data handling clarity
- Team transparency (who built this, who's responsible)
- Institutional language, not startup language

**For RuleSell specifically:**
- VirusTotal scanning on every uploaded asset
- Semgrep static analysis for code quality and security
- Sandboxed install testing
- Verified-install reviews (can't review what you haven't installed)
- Clear moderation process

**Design:** This section can afford to be denser — developers read. Use structured lists over marketing copy. Concrete claims over general reassurances.

**The test:** A skeptical developer should finish this section with their specific "what if this asset is malicious?" question answered.

---

### Section 10: Community / Ecosystem Section

**Business job:** Show that the product is alive, used, and growing. Dead products have no activity.

**What it needs:**
- Activity indicators: recent installs, recent reviews, new creators, recent updates
- Contributor counts or creator diversity
- Discussion or engagement metrics where authentic

**Design:** Convey vibrancy without manufactured energy. Don't fake metrics or show activity that isn't there. Real sparse activity is better than fake high activity — sophisticated developers will check.

**For RuleSell:** The Explore page serves this function. The landing page can reference it: "Join X developers exploring Y assets." Activity counts should be real or not shown.

---

### Section 11: CTA / Conversion Section

**Business job:** Catch high-intent visitors who scrolled to the bottom. They've read everything — they just need permission to commit.

**What it needs:**
- New angle from the hero (don't repeat the opening line)
- Brief value restatement — the single best reason
- Primary CTA, same as hero
- Optional: secondary action for not-yet-ready visitors (newsletter, docs, etc.)

**Design:** Natural conclusion — not desperate. The design should feel like the inevitable end of a journey, not a last-ditch plea. Can be more intimate in tone than the hero.

---

### Section 12: Footer

**Business job:** Navigation safety net and legal compliance. Also the place careful researchers look to evaluate legitimacy.

**What it needs:**
- Organized navigation: product links, company links, legal links
- Legal: Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Cookie Policy
- Social media links
- Company info: name, registration, contact

**Design:** Organized, not expansive. Dark background on dark sites creates a natural visual close. Column structure: Product | Company | Legal | Social. Never a link graveyard.

## Part 2: Page-Specific Architecture

### Marketplace Browse Page

**Primary job:** Help users find quickly and evaluate quality at a glance.

**Required sections, in order:**
1. Search bar — prominent position, autocomplete, placeholder text that conveys breadth ("Search 5,000+ rules, skills, and MCP servers")
2. Filter panel — category, platform (Cursor, Claude, Windsurf, etc.), environment, price (free/paid/any), collapsible on mobile
3. Sort controls — default to Quality Score descending, offer Newest, Most Installed, Price
4. Item grid — name, author, quality badge (prominent), price, platform tags, install count
5. Pagination or infinite scroll with clear count ("Showing 1–24 of 847 results")

**Design priorities:**
- Quality score should be the most visually prominent metric on each card
- Platform tags should be instantly scannable (small, colored, distinct)
- Empty/no-results states should guide the user to broaden their search, not just show "no results"
- Filters should never obscure results on mobile

**Empty state design:** "No results for X" should suggest: try these categories / remove these filters / here are trending items. Never a dead end.

---

### Marketplace Item Detail Page

**Primary job:** Convert a browser into an installer or buyer.

**Required sections, in order:**
1. Item header — name, author, quality badge (prominent), price, install CTA (platform-aware: "Install in Cursor")
2. Quality score breakdown — the scoring dimensions that produced the badge, not just the number
3. README / documentation — rendered markdown, the full docs
4. Reviews — verified-install only, with reviewer role, install date, platform used
5. Version history — changelog, version dates
6. Related items — from same creator, same category, or complementary
7. Creator profile sidebar — persistent on desktop, collapsible on mobile

**Design direction:** Feel like excellent documentation, not a sales page. Developers don't want to be sold to — they want to evaluate clearly. Put information density in service of the evaluator, not the seller.

**The install CTA:** Must be platform-aware. Detect or let the user select which tool they're installing into. Don't show a generic "download" — show "Install in Cursor" or "Add to Claude Code."

---

### Creator Dashboard

**Primary job:** Help creators understand how their items are performing and optimize accordingly.

**Required sections, in order:**
1. Revenue overview — total earnings, this period, pending payout, next payout date. Lead with the number they care about most.
2. Item performance — per-item install count, reviews, quality score, revenue
3. Analytics — install trends over time, geographic breakdown, referral sources
4. Listing management — edit, unpublish, update, add new
5. Affiliate earnings — separate from item revenue, clear performance metrics

**Design principles:**
- Lead with the number that matters (earnings). Progressive disclosure for everything else.
- Charts should be immediately comprehensible — don't require mental math to read a trend
- Empty states for new creators should motivate, not shame: "Your first install is one publish away"

---

### Landing Page / Homepage

**Primary job:** Convert first-time visitors who arrived from search, social, or word of mouth.

**Recommended section sequence for RuleSell:**

1. Hero — benefit headline, quality score visual, primary CTAs (Browse / Start Selling)
2. Social proof bar — metrics + logos
3. Problem statement — the fragmented, unverifiable state of AI rules today
4. How it works — buyer path + creator path (tabbed or sequential)
5. Differentiators — quality measurement, creator economics, security verification
6. Trust section — verification process, security
7. Community / Explore preview — activity signal
8. Bottom CTA — close with conviction

## Part 3: Conversion Flow Design

### Visitor → Buyer Flow

The five transitions that lead from arrival to completed purchase/install:

1. **Awareness → Interest:** Hero lands the value proposition. Visitor decides to keep reading. (Hero section does this job.)
2. **Interest → Consideration:** Visitor browses, finds a category or item. Quality scores differentiate options. (Browse + Quality badge does this job.)
3. **Consideration → Evaluation:** Visitor opens an item detail page. README, quality breakdown, and reviews answer the "is this actually good?" question. (Item detail does this job.)
4. **Evaluation → Intent:** Visitor decides this item solves their problem. Install CTA is clear, platform-specific, and low-friction. (Install CTA + pricing clarity does this job.)
5. **Intent → Completion:** Install or purchase completes. Confirmation is immediate and clear. Next step is obvious (rate, add more, share). (Post-purchase confirmation does this job.)

**Design the seams:** Each transition should feel seamless. The next step should be the obvious one, not a choice that requires effort.

---

### Creator → Seller Flow

1. **Awareness → Registration:** Creator hears about the platform (likely from a buyer). Landing page creator section shows the economics clearly. Signup is fast. (Creator landing section + simple signup does this job.)
2. **Registration → First Upload:** Onboarding guides the creator to their first item submission. Requirements are clear before upload, not discovered during. (Onboarding checklist / upload flow does this job.)
3. **First Upload → Verification:** Automated checks happen quickly. Creator knows their item is being scanned, not waiting in a black box. (Upload status + progress communication does this job.)
4. **Verification → Published:** Item passes or fails with clear feedback. Failures include actionable fix guidance. (Verification result page does this job.)
5. **Published → Earning:** Creator sees their item in the marketplace, tracks performance, receives first payout. The dashboard confirms the model works as promised. (Creator dashboard does this job.)

## Part 4: Responsive Design Architecture

Mobile isn't an afterthought — it's the surface many users discover products on. Design these section behaviors:

| Section | Mobile Behavior |
|---------|----------------|
| Hero | Full viewport height, shorter headline, CTA positioned in thumb-reachable zone (lower half of screen) |
| Social proof bar | Horizontal scroll with fade-out edges, or reduce to 3–4 most recognizable logos |
| Features | Stack vertically, image above text, full-width |
| How it works | Vertical steps, numbered list format, arrows become vertical connectors |
| Pricing | Cards stack vertically, highlighted card moves to top |
| Testimonials | Single-column, swipeable or vertically stacked |
| Browse page | Filters in a slide-out drawer (triggered by filter button), single-column item cards, search bar fixed at top |
| Item detail | Quality badge + install CTA sticky at bottom of screen |
| Creator dashboard | Revenue card full-width at top, chart scrollable, table switches to card view |
| Navigation | Bottom tab bar preferred over hamburger for 4 or fewer items. Hamburger only if necessary. |

**Breakpoints for this project:** Match Tailwind defaults (sm: 640px, md: 768px, lg: 1024px, xl: 1280px). Design at 375px (iPhone SE) and 1440px (desktop). Test everything in between.

## Part 5: Performance Requirements

Performance is a product quality issue, not just a technical one. Slow pages destroy trust faster than bad design.

**Core Web Vitals targets:**
- FCP (First Contentful Paint): < 1.5s
- LCP (Largest Contentful Paint): < 2.5s
- CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift): < 0.1
- FID / INP: < 200ms

**Implementation standards:**
- `font-display: swap` on all web fonts — no invisible text during load
- Maximum 2 font families per page (3 at absolute maximum)
- All images in WebP format with JPEG/PNG fallbacks
- Responsive `srcset` — don't serve 1200px images to mobile
- Lazy load below-fold images
- Preload LCP image (hero visual, above fold)
- No layout shift from late-loading images — always set width/height or aspect-ratio

**For Next.js:** Use the `Image` component. It handles WebP conversion, responsive srcset, and lazy loading. Do not use raw `<img>` tags for content images.

## Part 6: RuleSell-Specific Context

### What RuleSell Is

A marketplace for AI developer assets: MCP servers, skills (like this one), cursor rules, Claude rules, workflows, agent teams, prompts, CLIs, datasets, and bundles. Think npm for AI developer configuration — but with quality measurement baked in.

### The Differentiators (Design Should Reflect These)

1. **Quality is measured, not voted on** — every item receives a computed quality score based on token efficiency, install success rate, security scan results, and maintenance activity. Star ratings are supplementary. The quality score is the brand's visual identity.
2. **Creators keep 85%** — better economics than almost any comparable marketplace. This is a strong acquisition argument for creators and a trust signal for buyers (well-compensated creators maintain their work).
3. **Verified-install reviews only** — you cannot review an item you haven't installed. This prevents review manipulation and makes reviews genuinely useful.
4. **Cross-environment compatibility** — items declare which tools they work with (Cursor, Claude Code, Windsurf, Copilot, etc.), and compatibility is tested, not self-reported.

### Current Pages in Production
- Homepage
- Browse / Search
- Explore
- Leaderboard
- About
- Trust
- Affiliates
- (In progress) Item Detail, Creator Dashboard, Buyer Dashboard, Admin

### Target Audience

Developers using AI coding tools — primarily Cursor, Claude Code, GitHub Copilot, Windsurf users. They are:
- Technical, skeptical, quality-conscious
- Accustomed to evaluating npm packages, GitHub repos, documentation quality
- Will read before they act
- Value signal-to-noise ratio highly
- Respond to specificity over generality

### Design Personality

Three reference points held in tension:
- **Stripe documentation confidence** — dense information, trusted, nothing wasted, quality implicit in the craft
- **GitHub developer energy** — functional, direct, built for people who read code
- **Well-run package registry curation** — like npm or PyPI done right, with quality as a first-class citizen

Not: startup marketing, aggressive conversion tactics, or feature parades. Developers have strong smell for products trying too hard.

### Business Model Summary

| Actor | Economics |
|-------|-----------|
| Buyers | Free assets are free. Paid assets at listing price. |
| Creators | Keep 85% of paid asset revenue. Free to list. |
| Pro creators | $8/month for enhanced analytics, priority support, featured slots |
| Affiliates | 10% of referred transaction revenue |
| Platform | 15% commission on paid assets |

### Priority Page Order

1. Homepage (acquisition)
2. Browse (discovery + conversion)
3. Item Detail (conversion)
4. Creator Dashboard (creator retention)
5. Explore (engagement + SEO)
